Favorite–Longshot Bias — the refined v7 dimensions

After the v7 run, the 29-agent review, and the crypto/sports calibration check. Native fields, directional metric, mostly longitudinal.

0 · The measure (kept directional)

FLB stays exactly what the literature means: overpriced longshots (realized win-rate below price at low prices) and underpriced favorites (realized above price at high prices).

The statistic is the signed calibration slope b from a regression of realized outcome on price, won ~ a + b·price, estimated on a common price window across whatever buckets are being compared:

  • b > 1 → classic FLB (the calibration curve is steeper than 45°)
  • b < 1 → reverse FLB
  • b ≈ 1 → well-calibrated

The familiar D10−D1 decile spread stays only as an intuitive summary; the slope is the comparison statistic. We do not use an unsigned Brier/distance metric — it discards the direction, which is the entire phenomenon.

Why the change: the two-extreme-decile spread compares different price regions across buckets (a multi-outcome favorite sits below 0.5, a binary favorite above) and reads level offsets as tilts. The slope on common support is directional and comparable. Empirically it already paid off: Crypto b=1.02, Sports b=1.00 (well-priced); Politics/Finance/Geopolitics b≈1.08–1.10 (genuine FLB). The old "reverse-FLB crypto/sports" claim was a D10−D1 artifact and is dropped.

1 · The four new dimensions

Three are longitudinal (the only design that isolates learning rather than composition); one is the canonical horse-race form of FLB. All on native Polymarket fields, all scored with the signed slope.

repetition · longitudinal

① Series learning curve

Derivation. Use Polymarket's native series grouping (Gamma series_slug + recurrence). Within each recurring series, order its market instances by closed_time → instance index k = 1, 2, …. Compute the signed slope bk on each instance's trades, then regress bk on k (or on log prior-settled-count) with series fixed effects.
Why. Rules, resolution source, category and creator are constant within a series, so the only thing varying across k is elapsed repetitions + accumulated feedback. A slope of bk drifting toward 1 as k grows is learning — not a category mix. This is the genuine "do markets learn over reps" test; our cross-sectional dim_recurrence gave a clean gradient but couldn't separate learning from composition.
Caveat. In high-cadence series the instance index is near-collinear with calendar date — add calendar-period controls or stratify by cadence so "series aging" isn't confounded with "platform maturing."
individual learning · longitudinal · the missing half

② Trader experience curve

Derivation. For each trade, count the wallet's prior resolved trades strictly before that trade (trailing / point-in-time, never lifetime). Estimate the within-wallet relationship between the trade's calibration contribution and trailing experience, with wallet FE + calendar-quarter FE; report a within-vs-between decomposition and a reverse-time placebo (shuffle the wallet's own trade order — if the slope survives, it was composition, not learning).
Why. The only within-person learning test, and the half v6 never had (no trader axis at all). Trailing counts kill the look-ahead bias baked into lifetime-volume/PnL sorts (winners mechanically accumulate volume). Wallet FE + placebo separate genuine individual learning from platform maturation. Pairs with ① — market-level vs trader-level learning.
Sub-test. Domain-specific experience (prior trades in the same category/series) is sharper still, but needs a coverage check — few wallets are deep in one domain and naïve in another with enough tail mass.
anchorability · objective realized signal

③ Resolution ambiguity

Derivation. Primary = objective realized ambiguity from native fields: was the UMA resolution disputed (uma_statuses contains "disputed") or did it resolve 50/50 (tie/void). Ex-ante = an LLM-scored ambiguity rating of the description rules text (a ratings instrument, distinct from the discarded muddy classification; deterministic text features as validation). Always evaluated within category.
Why. This single dimension replaces the failed dim_anchor and the five brittle rules-text proxies. In the v7 run, the resolution_source ladder just re-expressed crypto-lottery structure (the price-feed bucket had the strongest FLB, not the weakest), and the review showed every text proxy is broken (conditional clauses disambiguate; "names a source" doesn't predict the realized 0.5-rate; everything routes through UMA anyway). The construct that actually survives is "is the outcome genuinely hard to adjudicate" — best measured by the objective dispute/0.5 outcome.
Caveat. A dispute is downstream of belief dispersion (a collider), so frame it as descriptive, conditioned on category + thickness; the ex-ante LLM-ambiguity score is the cleaner instrument for a causal reading.
complexity · the canonical FLB form

④ Within-event longshot bias

Derivation. Take native multi-outcome events (neg_risk = true — mutually-exclusive, one winner). Within each event, rank the outcomes by price and test whether the cheap (longshot) outcomes lose relative to their implied probability. Measure on the book mid-price (not last trade), with a minimum-liquidity threshold per outcome, and use liquidity as an identifying lever.
Why. This is FLB in its original Thaler–Ziemba horse-race form. Holding the event fixed differences out category, resolution source, liquidity regime and trader pool in one stroke — the best identification available, and the version the literature is actually about. It replaces the cross-sectional cardinality dim, which was a price-support/simplex artifact (multi-outcome favorites priced below 0.5, binaries above).
Caveat. A discrete bid-ask corrupts the price-of-record worst at the deep-longshot end — hence book-mid + min-liquidity. If the longshot tilt fades in liquid sub-samples it was microstructure; if it persists it's behavioral.

2 · Controls & companions (not learnability claims)

Controls — condition on these, don't headline them:

  • Market thickness — consolidate volume + liquidity + comment_count + competitive into one thickness/sophistication axis (use trade-time depth, not lifetime volume). The review showed these are all the same latent factor.
  • Category and horizon / time-to-resolution — covariates. The old "high-volume → public money reversal" is a named descriptive only; and since crypto/sports are now shown well-priced (b≈1), the reversal framing is retired.

Descriptive companions — report residualized, never as headline: the cross-sectional recurrence gradient; text_novelty (the algorithmic semantic-repetition angle on ①, audit-validated); the resolution_source anchor ladder.

4 · The throughline

22 muddy, cross-sectional, LLM-labelled slices → 4 clean dimensions — three of them longitudinal (the only way to actually isolate learning), one the canonical horse-race FLB — all on native Polymarket fields and a directional, artifact-resistant metric.

The thesis now rests on falsifiable, mechanism-specific questions: does FLB shrink within a recurring series, and within an individual trader, as repetitions accumulate? — plus the classic within-event test. And the empirical backdrop is now correct: the objective/liquid/repeated markets (crypto, sports) are simply well-priced, while the bias lives in the judgment-heavy categories — a sharper, more intuitive learnability story than the old public-money-reversal framing.
