Supports market-level learning. Novel series (no precedent) carry strong classic FLB (+0.053, t+7.9***); once a series has settled 100+ times it's gone, even mildly reversed (−0.014). The 0-vs-100+ contrast is clean and well-powered. (The 1–10 / 11–100 middle is non-monotone — noisier — so it's a 0-vs-established result more than a smooth dose-response.)

Raw gradient runs the WRONG way — and it's a confound, not a refutation. Novice wallets (0–9 prior trades) show ~zero FLB; experienced wallets (10+) carry significant FLB (+0.017 to +0.024, t up to +3.7). Taken literally that says experienced traders are more biased — but almost certainly because active/high-count wallets disproportionately trade the judgment-heavy markets (politics/finance) where FLB lives, while occasional traders stick to the popular, well-priced sports/crypto. This is composition, not skill. The clean test is the within-wallet version: wallet fixed effects + category controls + a reverse-time placebo, so we compare a trader to themselves early vs late rather than novices-vs-veterans across different markets. That's the next build.

Takeaway

Market-level learning is visible; trader-level learning is masked by composition in the raw cut. A recurring series de-biases as it matures (①) — the cleanest support yet for the learnability thesis on a native, leakage-free measure. The trader curve (②) can't be read at face value: the raw experience gradient is dominated by what experienced traders choose to trade. Until we run it within-wallet with category controls, ② is a confound demonstration, not evidence against individual learning.
