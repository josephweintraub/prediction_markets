Session audit

Critical review of everything done in this session: SII external validation · contract-migration fix + data extension to June 22 · "is newer data better calibrated" analysis · native tag→category taxonomy. What holds, what doesn't, and whether the categorical labelling is being done correctly.

Overall verdict. The infrastructure work is sound and well-verified (external validation, migration fix, dataset extension). The tag→category mapping is methodologically correct for its purpose, with fixable design flaws and one state-consistency problem. The weakest link is the headline scientific claim — "classic FLB has largely vanished in the newest data" — which is confounded by resolution censoring and should not be presented as-is.

2 · The biggest problem: resolution censoring in the "newest data is calibrated" claim

The May–Jun bucket is not a random sample of May–Jun markets — it's the subset that already resolved by the June 23 data cutoff. FLB rows require a resolution (won). A market that traded in May–June can only enter if it opened and resolved within ≤ 8 weeks. That mechanically selects hourly/daily/weekly recurring markets and excludes every long-horizon judgment market (elections, year-end props) — while their 2025 counterparts, all resolved by now, are in the 2025 bucket.

Our own numbers show the composition shift: Politics falls from n=2.22M trades (2025) to n=232K (May–Jun); Weather (hourly temperature series) becomes the largest May–Jun category at 3.35M; Esports+Iran+Weather+Sports — all fast-resolving — dominate the bucket. FLB has always concentrated in judgment/long-horizon markets, so comparing "all horizons, 2025" against "short-horizon only, 2026" can produce the 1.039 → 0.949 slope trend with zero actual change in behavior.

This also weakens the single most quotable result — "even Politics collapsed, 1.049 → 0.894". The May–Jun Politics cell is only short-horizon political markets (fast resolutions like confirmations, deadline votes), a very different animal from the 2025 cell that includes long-dated election markets.

The fix (cheap, one EC2 run)

Horizon-matched comparison: restrict every time bucket to markets with lifetime ≤ N days (e.g. 30), or compare the same recurring series across periods. If short-horizon 2025 markets also show slope ≈ 1.0, the "improvement" is composition, not learning — and the honest headline becomes "short-horizon markets are and were well-calibrated; the newest cohort's judgment markets can't be evaluated yet."

Secondary confounds in the same analysis

  • Stale bot list. wallet_flags was built on old data; May–Jun bot wallets created after the build aren't flagged. The newest bucket is precisely where high-frequency flow concentrates. Could contribute to the mild reverse tilt (0.949).
  • Up/down exclusion semantics differ across buckets. Old months exclude via eventSlug patterns; new months (sparse eventSlug) via outcome ∉ {Up,Down}. Threshold-style recurring crypto ("BTC above $X at 3pm?" — outcomes Yes/No) is up/down-family but survives the new filter. Quantify the leak: count May–Jun trades on markets carrying 5M/15M/1H or high-recurrence tags.
  • No external check on the V2 months. The SII validation covered the pre-migration pipeline. The V2-extracted May–Jun data has only been validated internally (invariants + overall calibration). One spot-check of May–Jun daily volume/trade counts against the Data-API or SII would close the loop.

3 · Is the categorical labelling being done correctly?

Short answer: yes at the mechanical level, with real design flaws to fix before it becomes "the" category variable for the paper.

3a · What the 98.7% actually measures (circularity)

The map was bootstrapped from P(LLM_category | tag), so the held-out 98.7% is agreement with the LLM labels, not accuracy against ground truth. Held-out across markets, but the same labeler generated train and test — it measures "tags can predict what the LLM would have said." That's the right consistency metric for a drop-in replacement, but it inherits LLM systematic errors. The market-level hand-checks are what break the circularity — independent judgment on question text found 0.3–1.3% error — and indeed several "disagreements" turned out to be LLM mistakes, not map mistakes. Caveat to keep in mind: the checkers are also LLM agents, so correlated blind spots are possible; for topic assignment (mostly human-obvious) this is acceptable.

3b · The volume-weighted number is the one that matters — and it's worse

Held-out agreement is 98.7% by count but 94.2% by volume: disagreements concentrate in large markets, and every FLB analysis is trade- or dollar-weighted. A 1% count error rate that lands on the top-volume markets can move a category's slope; the same rate on dust markets cannot. Cheapest high-value fix in this whole audit: hand-lock the top ~100 markets by volume to explicit categories (an hour of work, protects a huge share of analysis weight).

3d · No abstain bucket

The soft-vote takes an argmax no matter how thin the margin. The genuinely-ambiguous residual (crime = Culture-vs-Other, IPO = Tech-vs-Finance) currently gets a forced label and adds noise. Cleaner: assign "Mixed" below a vote-margin threshold and report its size. This also dissolves the "10 clean rounds" tension — ambiguous markets stop being classified as errors because they stop being classified.

3e · State inconsistency — needs fixing before anything ships

  • The FLB-by-category numbers were computed with a stale map. Timestamps prove it: native_flb_results.parquet 15:33 < final_tag_map.json 16:46 < precedence fixes 20:47+. So flb_newest_native.html predates the Mentions-precedence fix (~50/6000 Mentions markets misrouted into Politics/Culture at the time). Impact is small for big cells but the Mentions cell itself (n=35K) is undercounted — re-run with the finalized map before showing anyone.
  • Two round-2 fixes are documented but not baked into final_tag_map.json (demote Tweet Markets/Elon Tweets; remove Geopolitics precedence). They were verified against round-2's own errors — in-sample — so bake them and run one fresh confirmation round.
  • The canonical map lives in /tmp on both machines. /tmp is volatile; the hand-curation was expensive. Persist map + precedence rules + apply-logic into the repo as a module (e.g. analysis/learnability/native_categories.py + a versioned JSON) and commit.

3f · Missing statistics

The category × period cells report spread/slope with no clustered SEs or t-stats — the overall time-trend run had them, the native by-category run didn't. "Finance is the lone FLB holdout (1.13)" isn't a finding until it has a 3-way clustered SE, and 12 categories × 3 windows is a multiple-comparisons surface (the same discipline the v5 audit demanded of the learnability dims). Small cells (Economy n=21K, slope 0.738; Mentions n=35K) shouldn't be shown without them.

4 · Ops / hygiene loose ends

  • extract_orderfilled_v2.py and the build_trades memory bump are uncommitted. They are now load-bearing for the canonical 2.02B-row dataset. Commit them.
  • Alchemy API key hardcoded in pipeline/config.py. Move to an env var and rotate the key before any commit touches that file.
  • eventSlug is sparse on the new months — this silently breaks every slug/event-family-based dim in the learnability engine for post-April data, not just the up/down filter. Needs either a Gamma backfill join (token→event slug) or an explicit "dims end April" caveat on anything v6-style run over the extended data.
  • Writeup contradiction: flb_newer_data.html says "Politics and Finance kept their FLB" (2025+ cut, LLM labels) while flb_newest_native.html says "even Politics collapsed to 0.894" (May–Jun, native tags). Both are internally correct but a reader sees a contradiction; the reconciliation (time-split + the censoring problem above) should be stated wherever both are shown.

5 · Ranked recommendations

  1. Horizon-matched FLB comparison (kills or confirms the headline "newest data is calibrated" claim — do this before the claim travels).
  2. Bake the two map fixes → fresh confirmation round → freeze, then persist map + precedence rules into the repo and re-run the by-category FLB with the frozen map.
  3. Hand-lock the top ~100 markets by volume (attacks the 94.2%-by-volume weakness directly).
  4. Add 3-way clustered SEs to category × period cells; add an abstain/"Mixed" bucket below a vote-margin threshold.
  5. Commit the V2 extractor; de-hardcode + rotate the Alchemy key.
  6. Quantify the up/down-family leak in the May–Jun bucket and spot-check V2 months against an external source.
